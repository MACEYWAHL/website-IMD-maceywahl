Bunnies are considered EXOTIC pets and need specialized vets.

These vets are hard to find and more expensive. Bunnies can cost the same at the vet as cats and dogs. If you can't afford the vet, you can't afford the pet.

Common Health Concerns:

Conjunctivitis & The Snuffles

It is a bacterial infection causing many symptoms such as red eyes and lethargy. Vet assistance is required to treat. This is closely related to a respiratory infections called "snuffles". Symptoms are eye/nose discharge, red eyes and sneezing.

Learn more about Conjunctivitis

GI Stasis

This is a condition that occurs when a rabbit's diet does not have enough fiber. If not treated, this condition can be fatal for rabbits. Thankfully, feeding lots of hay (high in fiber) to your bunny is an effective preventive. Introducing too many new foods into a rabbit's diet can also cause GI Stasis in rabbits; if you want your bunny to try new things, do it one food at a time. You will know if your bunny has GI Stasis when they get bloated, sluggish, loses appetite, stops drinking fluids, and stops pooping.

Learn more on preventing GI Stasis

Dental Problems

Rabbit teeth grow continuously, so they need to chew on toys to wear them down. Chewing high-fiber foods, like hay, on a regular basis keeps teeth at a functional length. If your rabbit is chewing on something they shouldn't be, don't punish them as this is a natural and healthy behavior. Bunny-proofing your home is the answer to redirect this natural behavior to chew toys.

Learn More on Teeth

Bunnies are considered prey animals. They want to hide their injuries or illnesses from predators. Bunny ailments will not be presented as visibly and behavioraly as a cat or dog injury. Keep close watch on movements, eyes, teeth alignment, and intake of food and litter box usage.
